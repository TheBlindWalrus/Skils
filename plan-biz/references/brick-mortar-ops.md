# Brick-and-Mortar Operations Reference

Practical guidance for the daily operations of a small food retail business. Use this reference during the operations planning session (`04-operations`), the SOP development session (`05-setup`), and the ongoing operations review session (`06-launch`).

---

## Daily Operations: The Non-Negotiables

Every food retail business needs documented procedures for these before opening day. Undocumented procedures live in the owner's head — which means they disappear when the owner isn't there.

### Opening Procedure (template)

- [ ] Disarm alarm; turn on lights
- [ ] Check walk-in and display case temperatures (log them — health dept requires this)
- [ ] Pull yesterday's temperature log; verify overnight temps stayed in safe range
- [ ] Set out day's product from walk-in / production
- [ ] Check product rotation (FIFO — older product forward, newer in back)
- [ ] Check display case fill; pull anything past its use-by date
- [ ] Set up POS: start cash drawer, verify amounts, confirm receipt paper
- [ ] Turn on espresso machine; run two blank shots to flush; calibrate grind if needed
- [ ] Check cleaning schedule: what's due today?
- [ ] Check inventory for anything running low that needs to be ordered
- [ ] Open at scheduled time

### Closing Procedure (template)

- [ ] Stop taking new orders at posted close time
- [ ] Sell down or box remaining product; bag day-olds for employee purchase or donation
- [ ] Close out POS: run Z-report, count drawer, record totals
- [ ] Deposit or secure cash per your procedures
- [ ] Clean espresso machine: backflush, clean group heads, wipe steam wands
- [ ] Wash all equipment (mixing bowls, sheet pans, smallwares)
- [ ] Run three-compartment sink cycle for any remaining items
- [ ] Clean and sanitize all food contact surfaces
- [ ] Sweep and mop floor (do NOT mop before all dishes are done)
- [ ] Take out trash; replace liners
- [ ] Check temperature logs; ensure refrigeration is running properly before leaving
- [ ] Arm alarm; lock up

### Food Safety Temperature Logs

Required by health departments in most jurisdictions. Keep a physical log (a printed form in the kitchen) and check twice daily minimum.

| Equipment | Safe Range | Check Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Walk-in refrigerator | 35–38°F | Twice daily (open and close) |
| Reach-in display case | ≤41°F | Twice daily |
| Walk-in freezer | 0°F or below | Daily |
| Proof box | Per recipe (usually 75–85°F) | During use |
| Oven thermometer | ±10°F of set temp | Weekly calibration check |

**If a unit is out of range:** Do not sell product from it until you've confirmed the unit is back in range AND product hasn't been in the temperature danger zone (40°F–140°F) for more than 4 hours cumulative. When in doubt, throw it out — the cost of wasted product is always less than the cost of a customer illness.

### Cash Handling

Establish these procedures before you open and train every shift lead on them:

- **Starting drawer amount:** Define a consistent starting cash amount (e.g., $200 in change). Count and confirm every opening.
- **Mid-day drop:** When the drawer exceeds a certain amount (e.g., $500), pull the excess and secure it. Don't let cash accumulate.
- **End-of-day count:** Count drawer, compare to POS Z-report. Variance over $5 should be investigated.
- **Bank deposit:** Deposit cash daily or every other day. Don't let large amounts accumulate overnight on-site.
- **Two-person rule (if possible):** Have two people count the drawer at opening and closing; both sign the log. This protects both the business and the employees.

## POS Systems for Food Retail

Your POS system is the nervous system of your operation — it takes orders, processes payments, tracks sales, and provides the data you need to manage the business. Choose carefully.

### Comparison for Small Food Retail

| System | Best For | Monthly Cost | Hardware Cost | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Square for Restaurants** | Small cafés and counter-service bakeries; first-time operators | Free–$60/mo | $49–$799 | Easy setup, no contracts, free tier works for simple operations, strong reporting | Limited customization; can feel basic for complex operations |
| **Toast** | Growing restaurants and bakeries; planning to scale | $0–$165+/mo | $627–$1,000+ | Restaurant-specific features, kitchen display system, payroll integration, strong loyalty | Higher hardware cost; longer contracts; more complex |
| **Clover** | Retailers adding food service; flexible hardware options | $14–$95/mo | $49–$1,799 | Flexible hardware ecosystem; app marketplace for add-ons | Can get expensive with add-ons; some reports of poor customer service |
| **Lightspeed Restaurant** | Multi-location or complex menu operations | $69–$399/mo | Varies | Strong inventory and reporting; multi-location management | Overkill and expensive for a single small bakery |

**Recommendation for a new bakery:** Start with **Square for Restaurants** (free tier or Plus at $60/month). It covers everything you need at opening without lock-in. Upgrade to Toast when you have more than 5 employees and need kitchen display screens or deeper payroll integration.

### What your POS must handle from day one

- Credit/debit/mobile payments (Apple Pay, Google Pay)
- Itemized sales reporting (which items are selling, when, how much)
- End-of-day sales totals and cash reconciliation
- Basic customer receipt by email or SMS
- Refunds and voids

### What's nice to have (can add later)

- Online ordering integration
- Loyalty program
- Gift cards
- Employee time clock
- Kitchen display screen (for high-volume or multi-station operations)

## Inventory Management

Poor inventory management is one of the most common ways small food businesses lose money. Waste is directly subtracted from your margin.

### The FIFO Rule
**First In, First Out.** Older product always moves to the front; newer product goes behind it. Label everything with production date. Non-negotiable.

### Par Levels
A par level is the minimum quantity of each ingredient or product you want to have on hand at any time. When inventory drops to par, you reorder.

Setting par levels:
1. Calculate your average weekly usage of each ingredient
2. Add your reorder lead time (how many days until delivery arrives)
3. Add a safety buffer (10–20% extra)
4. That's your par level

Example: You use 50 lbs of bread flour per week. Delivery takes 2 days. Par = (50 ÷ 7) × 2 days lead + 15% buffer = ~16 lbs. When you're at 16 lbs, order more.

### Weekly Ordering Cycle
Most bakeries order ingredients 1–2 times per week. Establish a consistent ordering day (e.g., every Monday for Wednesday delivery). This:
- Reduces decision fatigue
- Makes it easier to manage cash flow (predictable cash outflows)
- Helps you spot trends (are you ordering more flour? You're selling more bread)

### Waste Tracking
Track what you throw away. By category:
- **Production waste:** Batches that failed, shaping errors, burnt items
- **Spoilage:** Finished product that expired before sale
- **Customer comps:** Mistakes that you replaced for free

Waste above 8–10% of production is a problem. Identify the cause: recipe issues, over-production, equipment problems, staff training gaps.

### Simple Inventory Sheet
A Google Sheet tracking sheet works fine for a small bakery:

## Supplier Sourcing

Your ingredients define your product. Supplier relationships matter.

### Distributor vs. Direct

**National / regional distributors** (Sysco, US Foods, Gordon Food Service):
- Pros: One invoice, one delivery, wide selection, consistent availability
- Cons: Not always the best quality for specialty items; minimums can be high; less relationship-driven pricing
- Best for: Commodity items (butter, eggs, salt, sugar, flour, dairy), packaging, disposables (to-go boxes, napkins, bags)
- Getting started: Request a sales rep visit; they'll come to you, walk through your needs, and set you up with an account

**Local and regional suppliers:**
- Pros: Fresher product, shorter supply chain, better story for marketing, often willing to do smaller minimums
- Cons: Multiple invoices and deliveries, less reliability at scale, may have seasonal gaps
- Best for: Specialty flours, fresh produce, specialty dairy, seasonal ingredients, anything you want to market as local
- Finding them: Ask at farmers markets, contact your state's department of agriculture for local food directories, talk to other restaurants in your area

**Specialty suppliers:**
- Chocolate and cocoa: Valrhona, Cacao Barry, Callebaut (all have distributor networks)
- Specialty flours: King Arthur (direct and through distributors), Hayden Flour Mills, regional mills
- Extracts and flavors: Nielsen-Massey (vanilla), Amoretti (specialty)
- Specialty packaging: Nashville Wraps, Paper Mart, Uline (for boxes, bags, tissue)

### Negotiating Terms

When you're starting out, most suppliers will require prepayment or COD. As you build a payment history, negotiate:
- **Net 7 or Net 15 terms** (pay within 7–15 days of invoice rather than on delivery)
- **Volume discounts** (ask at what volume a lower price applies)
- **Price holds** (locking in pricing for 3–6 months on commodity items like butter and flour, which fluctuate)

## Online Ordering & Delivery

### In-store pickup via your own platform
The highest-margin option. Customers order on your website; you fulfill in-store.

- **Square Online** (integrated with Square POS): Free for basic; pickup orders automatically route to POS
- **ChowNow**: ~$150/month flat fee; no commission per order; better for higher-volume operations
- **Toast Online Ordering**: Built into Toast POS; straightforward if you're already on Toast

### Third-party delivery platforms
Higher volume reach but significantly lower margin.

| Platform | Commission Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| DoorDash | 15–30% | Largest reach; can negotiate rate with higher commitment |
| Uber Eats | 15–30% | Second largest; strong in urban markets |
| Grubhub | 15–25% | Third; declining market share |

**Commission reality check:** If your average ticket is $15 and a platform takes 25%, you net $11.25 — before COGS and labor. Many bakeries find third-party delivery unprofitable on their core products. Consider:
- Adding a platform-specific markup (many platforms allow this; some customers expect it)
- Limiting delivery to higher-margin items
- Using platforms only for customer acquisition, not as a primary revenue channel
- Offering your own delivery for large orders at a flat fee

## Loyalty Programs

Loyal customers are your most valuable customers. The top 20% of customers at most food businesses generate 50–80% of revenue.

### Options for a small bakery

| Tool | Cost | Strengths |
|---|---|---|
| **Square Loyalty** | $45/mo | Integrated with Square POS; no separate app needed for customers; seamless |
| **Stamp Me** | Free–$30/mo | Digital punch card; simple for customers; app-based |
| **Fivestars** | $200–$400/mo | More robust; automated campaigns; better analytics | Overkill for small operations |
| **Email list + manual rewards** | Free | Simplest; no tech friction; very small operations often start here |

**What actually builds loyalty:**
1. Consistent quality (people return for the thing they know will be good)
2. Staff who remember regulars (their name, their usual order)
3. Surprise and delight (a free cookie sample, a "thank you for being a regular" note in a big order)
4. Being genuinely part of the community (showing up at local events, knowing the neighborhood)

Technology amplifies loyalty; it doesn't create it. Start with great product and genuine relationships before investing in a loyalty platform.

## Health Department Ongoing Compliance

Passing your opening inspection is not a one-time event. Health departments conduct unannounced routine inspections, typically 1–3 times per year depending on your risk category.

### What keeps you in good standing

**Temperature logs:** Keep them current, accurate, and accessible. An inspector who asks to see logs and finds they're missing or clearly fabricated will escalate the issue.

**Cleaning schedule:** Post a visible cleaning schedule showing daily, weekly, and monthly tasks. When the inspector asks "when was this floor drain last cleaned?", you want to be able to point to a log.

**Pest control:** Contract with a licensed pest control company for monthly preventive service. Keep their service reports on file. One mouse sighting during an inspection can result in immediate closure.

**Employee health policy:** You are required to have a policy that prohibits sick employees from working with food. Document it in your employee handbook and enforce it. An inspector may ask about your policy.

**Allergen management:** Post allergen information for all menu items where staff and customers can see it. Train all staff on the top 9 allergens and on cross-contamination prevention.

### Common violations that trigger re-inspection or closure

- **Critical violations (can result in immediate closure):**
  - Temperature abuse (product held in danger zone 40°F–140°F for >4 hours)
  - Evidence of pest activity (droppings, live insects, rodent tracks)
  - Improper handwashing / no handwashing soap or paper towels at hand sink
  - Employee with visible illness handling food
  - Sewage backup or plumbing failure

- **Non-critical violations (require correction at next inspection):**
  - Missing or expired food handler certifications
  - Unlabeled or improperly stored food
  - Equipment not NSF-certified
  - Missing temperature logs
  - Damaged or non-cleanable surfaces

### What to do when an inspector arrives

- Be calm and cooperative
- Give them immediate access
- Don't argue with findings on the spot — ask for clarification and take notes
- Correct anything you can correct immediately (wash a dish, add a label, etc.)
- Any violation they write up will specify a correction deadline; meet it

## Quality Control Systems

Consistency is your brand. A customer who loves your croissant on Tuesday needs to love it just as much on Saturday — made by a different baker under different conditions.

### Recipe standardization (before opening)

Every item on your menu needs a **standardized recipe card:**
- Ingredients listed by weight (not volume — "2 cups of flour" varies; "250 grams of flour" does not)
- Step-by-step process with specific temperatures and times
- Expected yield (how many units per batch)
- Photo of finished product (so staff know what "right" looks like)
- Key quality checkpoints (dough should look like this, feel like this at this stage)

### Batch testing before launch

Before adding any item to your permanent menu:
1. Make it 3 times, adjusting until you're satisfied
2. Standardize the recipe
3. Have 3–5 people taste and evaluate it (not just people who love you)
4. Calculate actual COGS against the standardized recipe
5. Confirm the item meets your pricing targets

### Ongoing quality monitoring

- **Daily tasting:** Taste the first item from each batch before it goes in the case. You'll notice issues before customers do.
- **Customer feedback loop:** Train staff to flag specific feedback ("someone said the lemon tart was too sweet today"). Log it.
- **Waste as a signal:** An item generating more waste than expected may have a quality or popularity issue. Investigate.
- **Regular recipe audits:** Every 6 months, taste your whole menu with fresh eyes. Are standards slipping? Is an item underperforming?
