# Food Service Legal & Regulatory Reference

Practical guidance for launching a food retail business in the United States. Laws vary significantly by state and municipality — use this as a starting map, then verify specifics with your county health department and a local business attorney.

---

## Business Formation

**Recommended structure: LLC**

An LLC (Limited Liability Company) separates your personal assets from business liability. For a food business — where a customer illness could trigger a lawsuit — this protection is non-negotiable.

- **Cost:** $50–$500 to file with your state's Secretary of State (varies widely)
- **Timeline:** 1–4 weeks for processing; expedited filing usually available for a fee
- **What you need:** Business name, registered agent (can be yourself), Articles of Organization
- **EIN:** Get a free Employer Identification Number from IRS.gov immediately after forming the LLC — you'll need it for taxes, banking, hiring, and many permit applications
- **DBA (Doing Business As):** If your LLC name differs from your store name, file a DBA (also called a fictitious business name) with your county clerk. Usually $25–$100.

**S-Corp election:** Not necessary at launch. Revisit with a CPA once revenue exceeds ~$50K/year — there can be tax advantages at that point.

**Sole proprietor:** Do not launch a brick-and-mortar food business as a sole proprietor. You have unlimited personal liability.

**Finding your state's filing portal:** Search "[your state] Secretary of State business filing" or go to your state's official .gov website and look for Business/Corporations.

## Business License & Local Permits

Every city or county requires a general business license before you operate. Requirements and costs vary widely.

- **Where to get it:** City clerk's office or county clerk, or increasingly through an online portal. Search "[your city] business license application."
- **Cost:** $50–$500/year typically
- **Timeline:** 1–4 weeks; some cities issue same-day online
- **Renewal:** Usually annual

**Additional local permits to check for:**
- Home occupation permit (if operating from home initially)
- Sign permit (required before installing exterior signage; often involves city planning department)
- Sidewalk café / outdoor seating permit (if applicable; separate from your main business license)
- Alarm permit (if you install a security system)

## Food Service Establishment Permit

This is the primary operating permit for any business that prepares or serves food to the public. Issued by your county or city health department.

**What it covers:** Authorizes your specific location to operate as a food service establishment. It is tied to your address — if you move, you apply again.

**How to get it:**
1. Contact your county health department (call, don't just read the website — requirements change and staff can answer questions about your specific concept)
2. Submit a plan review application with your proposed floor plan, equipment list, and menu before buildout begins. The health department must approve your layout before you build.
3. Complete buildout to health department specifications
4. Schedule a pre-opening inspection
5. Pass inspection → permit issued

**Timeline:** Plan review typically takes 2–6 weeks. Pre-opening inspection scheduling can add 1–4 weeks. **Start this process the moment you have a signed lease.**

**Cost:** $200–$1,500+ depending on jurisdiction and establishment type

**What inspectors look for:**
- NSF-certified commercial equipment (not residential appliances)
- Three-compartment sink (wash, rinse, sanitize) — almost universally required
- Dedicated hand-washing sink in the food preparation area (not the same as the utility sink)
- Grease trap or interceptor if using fryers or heavy cooking equipment
- Appropriate ventilation and hood systems (check local code — thresholds vary)
- Flooring: smooth, non-absorbent, cleanable (tile or sealed concrete; no carpet in prep areas)
- Adequate refrigeration with thermometers
- Pest exclusion (sealed walls, covered drains)
- Proper lighting levels in prep areas

## Food Handler Certifications

Most states require at least one certified food protection manager on staff (often the owner/manager). Many require all food handlers to have a basic food handler card.

**Food Protection Manager Certification (e.g., ServSafe):**
- Required in most states for the person in charge
- Takes 1–2 days of study + a proctored exam
- Cost: ~$15–$40 for study materials + $36 for the exam
- Valid for 5 years
- Where to get it: ServSafe.com (National Restaurant Association), or state-equivalent programs (some states have their own — check with your health dept)

**Food Handler Card (basic):**
- Required for all food service workers in many states
- Typically a 2–4 hour online course + short quiz
- Cost: $7–$15/employee
- Valid for 2–3 years
- Enroll all new hires before their first shift handling food

**Texas note:** Texas requires a Food Manager Certification AND a Food Handler Certificate for all food employees. Check your specific state's requirements.

## Certificate of Occupancy (CO)

A CO confirms that a space is legally approved for its intended use. Required before you open to the public.

- **Who issues it:** Your city or county building department
- **When you need it:** After buildout is complete and all required inspections are passed (building, electrical, plumbing, fire, health)
- **Timeline:** Can take 2–8 weeks after all inspections pass; delays are common
- **What triggers the need for a new CO:** Change of use (e.g., a former retail space becoming a food service space always requires a new CO)

**Important:** Do not plan to open before you have the CO in hand. Occupying a space without one is illegal and can result in forced closure.

## Zoning & Conditional Use Permits

Zoning laws determine what types of businesses can operate in which locations.

- **Retail food service** is typically permitted in commercial/mixed-use zones. Confirm before signing a lease.
- **Home-based food production** may be permitted in residential zones under cottage food laws (see below), but opening a public-facing retail bakery from a home requires a change of use — typically not permitted in residential zones.
- **Conditional Use Permit (CUP):** Some uses (drive-through, outdoor seating, late-night hours, alcohol) require a CUP even in commercial zones. CUPs go through a public process and can take 60–180 days. Don't assume — ask the planning department.

**Always verify zoning before signing a lease.** Ask your landlord, then verify independently with the city/county planning department.

## Cottage Food Laws (Home Production)

If you're starting small and producing from home before opening a storefront:

- **What's typically allowed:** Baked goods, jams, jellies, candy, dried goods, and other non-hazardous shelf-stable products
- **What's typically excluded:** Anything requiring refrigeration (cream fillings, cheesecake, meat), canned low-acid foods
- **Direct-to-consumer only:** Most cottage food laws require you to sell directly to the end consumer (farmers market, home delivery, your website). No selling to retail stores or restaurants in most states.
- **Sales caps:** Vary by state — from $5,000/year (restrictive) to $75,000+/year (permissive) to no cap
- **Labeling requirement:** Usually required to label products with your name, address, and "made in a home kitchen not inspected by the [state] Department of Agriculture"

Cottage food is a valid starting point for testing your product and building customers before committing to a commercial space. Not a permanent operating model for a retail storefront.

## If You Plan to Wholesale or Sell CPG Products

Selling packaged goods to retail stores, restaurants, or across state lines triggers additional requirements:

**Licensed commercial kitchen:** Your home kitchen (even cottage-food-permitted) is not sufficient for wholesale production in most states. Options:
- Rent a licensed shared commercial kitchen ($15–$40/hour)
- Use a co-packer (contract manufacturer who produces under your brand)
- Open your own licensed commercial kitchen (requires separate permitting)

**FDA Facility Registration:** Required if you sell food products in interstate commerce (across state lines). Free registration at FDA.gov. Bona fide retail bakeries selling in-person are typically exempt; packaged goods shipped to customers in other states are not.

**FDA Nutrition Labeling (21 CFR Part 101):** Required on all packaged food products. Must include:
- Product name
- Net weight
- Ingredients list (descending order by weight)
- Nutrition Facts panel
- Top 9 allergen declarations (peanuts, tree nuts, milk, eggs, wheat, soy, fish, shellfish, sesame)
- Manufacturer name and address
- Country of origin

Small business exemption: businesses with fewer than 100 employees and under 100,000 units sold per year qualify for simplified labeling. Still requires allergen declarations.

**USDA jurisdiction:** If your products contain more than 2% cooked meat or 3% raw meat, USDA (not FDA) has jurisdiction. Bakeries: this typically applies if you add meat fillings (quiche, meat pies, etc.).

## Product Liability Insurance

**Non-negotiable before opening day.**

If a customer gets sick from your food, you will be named in a lawsuit regardless of whether you were negligent. Product liability insurance covers this.

- **Minimum recommended:** $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate
- **Annual cost:** $500–$1,500/year for a small food business (varies by revenue and risk level)
- **Where to get it:**
  - **FLIP (Food Liability Insurance Program)** — flip.insure — specifically designed for food businesses; approximately $299–$500/year for small operations
  - **Next Insurance** — nextinsurance.com — online quote in minutes
  - **Your state farm bureau** — often has competitive rates for food businesses
  - **Independent commercial insurance broker** — best for bundling with property and workers comp

**Bundle with:**
- Commercial property insurance (covers your equipment, inventory, and buildout if fire, theft, or natural disaster occurs)
- Business interruption insurance (covers lost income if you're forced to close temporarily)
- Workers compensation insurance (required by law once you have employees in most states; often required even for part-time employees)

## Employment Law Basics

**W-2 employees vs. 1099 contractors:** Food service workers — bakers, counter staff, cooks — are almost always employees (W-2), not contractors. The IRS has strict tests for contractor classification, and misclassification carries significant penalties. Do not classify regular staff as contractors.

**Minimum wage:** Federal minimum is $7.25/hour, but most states and many cities have higher minimums. Check your state and city — many food industry hotspots have $15–$20/hour minimums.

**Tip rules:** If your counter staff receive tips, understand your state's tip credit rules. Some states allow a lower "tipped minimum wage"; others require full minimum wage plus tips. California, for example, has no tip credit — all employees earn full minimum wage regardless of tips.

**Overtime:** Non-exempt employees (most hourly food workers) earn 1.5x their regular rate for hours over 40/week under federal law. Some states have daily overtime rules.

**Hiring paperwork for every new employee:**
- I-9 (Employment Eligibility Verification) — must be completed within 3 business days of hire
- W-4 (Federal tax withholding)
- State withholding form
- Direct deposit authorization
