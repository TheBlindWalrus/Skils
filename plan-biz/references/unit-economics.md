# Unit Economics & Financials Reference

Practical financial guidance for a brick-and-mortar food business. Use this reference during the strategy session (02), concept session (03), and the financial review session (06).

---

## The Four Numbers That Drive Everything

Before building any model, internalize these four percentages. Every menu price, staffing decision, and location choice flows through them.

| Line Item | Target % of Revenue | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Food cost (COGS) | 28–35% | Higher for specialty/organic; lower for simple baked goods |
| Labor cost | 28–35% | Includes your own pay if you're working in the store |
| Occupancy (rent + utilities) | 8–12% | Rent alone should be ≤10% |
| Remaining for other expenses + profit | 18–36% | Marketing, supplies, insurance, repairs, debt service, profit |

**The brutal math:** If food cost is 32%, labor is 32%, and rent is 12%, you've already spent 76 cents of every dollar. You have 24 cents left for everything else — insurance, marketing, equipment repairs, loan payments, and whatever you take home. There is very little fat in food service. Every percentage point matters.

## COGS Calculation for Baked Goods

COGS (Cost of Goods Sold) = everything it costs to make one unit of what you sell.

### Components of COGS

1. **Ingredients** — actual cost of flour, butter, eggs, sugar, add-ins, etc. for one batch, divided by units produced
2. **Packaging** — box, bag, tissue paper, sticker, twine — everything that goes out the door with the product
3. **Direct labor** — time to make, shape, proof, bake, and finish the item × your labor cost per hour (include payroll taxes: add ~15% to hourly wage)
4. **Facility allocation** — your share of kitchen rent/overhead per batch (monthly kitchen cost ÷ batches per month)
5. **Utilities** — estimated cost of gas/electricity per bake
6. **Waste factor** — add 5–10% to account for failed batches, mistakes, unsold items

### Sample COGS calculation: one dozen croissants

| Component | Calculation | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Ingredients | Flour, butter, milk, eggs, salt, yeast per dozen | $2.80 |
| Packaging | Box + tissue paper | $0.65 |
| Direct labor | 45 min @ $20/hr × 1.15 payroll tax ÷ 2 dozen per batch | $4.31 |
| Facility | $1,800/mo kitchen ÷ 600 batches/mo = $3/batch ÷ 2 dozen | $1.50 |
| Utilities | Estimated $0.40/dozen | $0.40 |
| Waste (8%) | | $0.77 |
| **Total COGS** | | **$10.43/dozen** |

**Food cost %:** If you sell the dozen for $30, food cost = $10.43/$30 = **34.8%** — right at target.

### Shortcuts and rules of thumb

- **Simple baked goods (cookies, muffins, quick breads):** COGS typically 20–28% of selling price; labor component is lower
- **Laminated doughs (croissants, danishes, puff pastry):** COGS 32–38%; labor-intensive
- **Cakes and custom decorated items:** COGS 30–40%; high labor, must price accordingly
- **Coffee drinks:** COGS 20–28%; coffee is very high margin; add pastry sales to the same transaction for blended margin improvement
- **Savory items (quiche, sandwiches):** COGS 28–35%; typically more ingredient cost, faster labor

## Menu Pricing Method

### The food cost percentage method

`Selling price = COGS ÷ target food cost %`

**Example:** If your COGS for a croissant is $0.87 and you want a 30% food cost:
`$0.87 ÷ 0.30 = $2.90 minimum selling price`

### Reality check: competitive benchmarking

After calculating your cost-based price, check what comparable businesses charge. If your math says $2.90 but every other artisan bakery in your market charges $3.50–$4.50 for a croissant, **price to the market, not just to your costs** — you're leaving money on the table.

If your math says $4.50 but the market charges $2.75, you have a cost problem to solve, not a pricing problem. Either reduce COGS (bigger batches, different sourcing, simpler recipe) or reframe your wedge as premium enough to justify the premium price.

### Psychological pricing

- **Whole dollars outperform cents for baked goods:** $3.50 feels better than $3.47; $4.00 feels better than $3.95 at a bakery counter
- **Bundles shift average transaction value:** "Dozen for $32, half dozen for $18" (buying the dozen saves $4; most people buy the dozen)
- **Specials and seasonal items:** Can carry premium pricing (15–25% above similar regular items); novelty justifies it

### Pricing by channel

If you sell through multiple channels, margin varies significantly:

| Channel | % of retail price you keep | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Your storefront (direct) | 100% | Best margin; your fixed cost base |
| Catering (direct) | 90–100% | Lower counter labor; higher packaging/transport |
| Wholesale to restaurants | 50–60% | Lower margin; higher volume; no retail labor |
| Wholesale to grocery | 40–50% | Lowest margin; requires significant volume to matter |
| Delivery apps (DoorDash, etc.) | 70–75% | Platform takes 15–30%; consider adding surcharge |
| Online shipping (your site) | 85–90% | Shipping offsets some margin loss vs. in-store |

## Break-Even Analysis

Before you open, calculate your break-even: the revenue you need each month to cover all costs.

### Step 1: Calculate fixed monthly costs

| Cost | Estimate |
|---|---|
| Rent (NNN total) | $X,XXX |
| Utilities (electric, gas, water) | $XXX |
| Base staff labor (minimum viable schedule) | $X,XXX |
| Your own minimum salary | $X,XXX |
| Loan payment (if applicable) | $XXX |
| Insurance (all types, monthly) | $XXX |
| POS / software subscriptions | $XXX |
| Accounting / payroll service | $XXX |
| Other fixed | $XXX |
| **Total fixed monthly** | **$XX,XXX** |

### Step 2: Calculate your contribution margin

`Contribution margin = 1 - food cost %`

If food cost is 32%, contribution margin = 68%. Each dollar of revenue contributes $0.68 toward covering fixed costs.

### Step 3: Calculate break-even revenue

`Break-even revenue = Fixed monthly costs ÷ Contribution margin`

**Example:** $15,000 fixed costs ÷ 0.68 = **$22,059/month break-even**

### Step 4: Translate to transactions

`$22,059 ÷ $8.50 average ticket = 2,595 transactions/month = 87 transactions/day (30 days)`

Does 87 transactions per day feel achievable given your concept, location, and target customer? If it doesn't, you need to either:
- Reduce fixed costs (negotiate lower rent, defer hiring)
- Increase average ticket (add coffee, add bundles, add premium items)
- Find a higher-volume location

## Cash Flow for Seasonal/Holiday-Heavy Food Businesses

Bakeries are highly seasonal. February, November–December, and spring (Mother's Day, Easter) drive disproportionate revenue. January and summer can be significantly slower.

### The cash flow gap problem

1. You buy ingredients before you sell the product
2. Custom order customers pay on delivery, not on order placement
3. Wholesale buyers typically pay net 15–30 (you invoice; they pay weeks later)
4. Your fixed costs (rent, labor) don't flex with slow weeks

**Mitigation:**
- **Require deposits on large custom orders** (50% upfront is standard for wedding cakes, large catering orders)
- **Run net-0 or net-7 terms with wholesale buyers, not net-30**, especially early on
- **Maintain a 2–3 month fixed-cost reserve** — don't spend that cash even in good months
- **Don't over-buy inventory** — waste and spoilage are silent margin killers; buy what you'll use in 5–7 days

### Seasonal planning

- **Build up your cash reserve during holiday peaks** (November–December)
- **Pre-sell and pre-take-deposits for holiday orders** starting 6–8 weeks out
- **Plan reduced hours or days during your slowest periods** — labor is your most controllable cost
- **Use slow periods for training, recipe development, catering outreach** — not as an excuse to spend

## Startup Cost Estimates

### Lean launch (existing food service space, minimal buildout)

| Item | Range |
|---|---|
| LLC formation + EIN + DBA | $100–$600 |
| Business license + permits | $500–$2,000 |
| Product liability + property insurance (first year) | $1,500–$3,500 |
| Light buildout / cosmetic improvements | $5,000–$20,000 |
| Equipment (used, basic) | $10,000–$25,000 |
| Initial inventory (ingredients + packaging) | $2,000–$5,000 |
| POS system | $500–$2,000 |
| Signage + basic branding | $1,500–$4,000 |
| Working capital reserve (3 months fixed costs) | $15,000–$45,000 |
| **Total** | **$36,100–$107,100** |

### Standard launch (shell space or moderate buildout)

| Item | Range |
|---|---|
| All of the above | $36,100–$107,100 |
| Full buildout (plumbing, ventilation, flooring) | $40,000–$120,000 |
| New commercial equipment | $20,000–$60,000 |
| Professional branding and signage | $3,000–$10,000 |
| Working capital reserve (increase to 6 months) | +$15,000–$45,000 |
| **Total** | **$114,100–$342,100** |

The variance is large because location, market, and concept dramatically affect buildout costs. Get real contractor quotes before finalizing your funding plan.

## Funding Options

Listed in recommended order for most small food businesses.

### Bootstrap (self-funded)
- **Best for:** Concepts starting small (pop-up, farmers market, cottage food) with a clear path to profitability before needing a full storefront
- **Pros:** No debt, no investors, full control
- **Cons:** Growth limited by personal capital

### Friends & Family
- **Best for:** Founders with a strong personal network and a clear concept
- **Terms:** Often informal; always document in writing (even with family) to protect the relationship
- **Risk:** Damages relationships if the business struggles

### SBA 7(a) Loan
- **What it is:** Government-backed small business loan through a participating bank or credit union. Maximum $500,000 for working capital; $5M for real estate/equipment.
- **Interest rate:** Prime + 2.25–4.75%; typically 10–12% currently (2024–2025)
- **Term:** Up to 10 years for working capital; up to 25 years for real estate
- **Requirements:** 2+ years in business (challenging for startups), OR owner has relevant industry experience; personal guarantee; business plan; 3 years personal tax returns; financial projections
- **Timeline:** 30–90 days from application to funding
- **Best resource:** SBA.gov → "Find a Lender"; also look for SBA Preferred Lenders in your area for faster processing

### SBA 504 Loan
- **What it is:** Specifically for real estate and equipment (not working capital). Typically covers up to 40% of project costs at a fixed low rate.
- **Best for:** Purchasing a building or major equipment ($100K+)
- **Requires:** Partnering with a Certified Development Company (CDC)

### USDA Business & Industry (B&I) Loan Guarantee
- **What it is:** USDA backs loans for businesses in rural areas and small towns (under 50,000 population).
- **Best for:** Founders in smaller markets that may not qualify for standard SBA
- **Find it:** USDA.gov → Rural Development → Business Programs

### CDFI (Community Development Financial Institution)
- **What it is:** Mission-driven lenders focused on underserved communities and small businesses that may not qualify for traditional bank loans.
- **Best for:** Founders in lower-income areas, minority-owned businesses, or those with limited credit history
- **Examples:** Opportunity Fund, Accion Opportunity Fund, Local Enterprise Assistance Fund (LEAF)
- **Find CDFIs in your area:** cdfi.us or oppertunityfinance.net

### Grants
- **USDA Value-Added Producer Grant:** For agricultural producers adding value (a bakery using local grain qualifies in some cases). Up to $250K.
- **State small business development grants:** Many states have food and agriculture-specific grant programs. Search "[your state] small business grant food" and "[your state] department of agriculture business development."
- **Demographic-specific:** Grants for women-owned, minority-owned, and veteran-owned food businesses exist at national and state levels. Search "food business grant [your demographic] [your state]."
- **Pros:** Free money; no repayment
- **Cons:** Competitive; often slow (3–12 months to award); specific use requirements

### Equity / Outside Investors
- **Not recommended until revenue exceeds $100K/year.** Giving up equity in a food business at early stage typically undervalues the business significantly.
- **If you go this route:** A local angel investor with food/retail industry experience is worth more than money. Their network and operational knowledge matter.
