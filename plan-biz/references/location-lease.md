# Location Scouting & Lease Reference

Practical guidance for finding the right space and negotiating a commercial lease for a food service business. The right space for the wrong price can kill a business before it opens; the wrong space at any price is worse. Use this reference during the location-scouting session in `04-operations`.

---

## What to Look for in a Location

### The non-negotiables (verify before spending time on anything else)

1. **Zoning permits your use.** Retail food service must be allowed in the zone. Call the city/county planning department with the address and your intended use. Don't rely on the landlord's word.
2. **Health department pre-approval is feasible.** Before signing, schedule a pre-approval site visit with your county health department. They will walk the space with you and identify what would need to change to pass inspection. This visit is free or low-cost and prevents expensive surprises.
3. **Grease trap / ventilation is in place or can be added.** If your concept involves anything fried or heavily cooked, the space needs an existing grease trap or the infrastructure to install one. This can cost $5,000–$20,000+ to add and may require landlord approval and city permits. Confirm before negotiating.
4. **Three-phase electrical (if needed).** Commercial ovens and certain equipment require 3-phase electrical service. Not all retail spaces have it. Adding it is expensive.
5. **Loading access.** You'll receive ingredient deliveries regularly. Confirm there's a service entrance or reasonable delivery access.

### The good signs
- **Existing food service use.** A space that previously housed a restaurant or bakery often has the grease trap, ventilation, three-compartment sink, and health dept approval already in place. This can save 4–16 weeks and $20,000–$80,000 in buildout costs.
- **Foot traffic that matches your daypart.** A morning bakery needs foot traffic at 7–10am. Visit the space at that time on a weekday and a weekend. Count people. Note what they're doing.
- **Complementary neighbors.** A coffee shop next to a bookstore does better than one next to another coffee shop. What businesses would drive your customers past your door?
- **Parking or transit access.** Depends on your concept and target customer. A neighborhood bakery serving walk-in regulars needs different access than a destination bakery people drive to.

### The red flags
- **Below-market rent with no explanation.** Ask why the previous tenant left. Ask how long it's been vacant.
- **Visible water damage, mold, or pest evidence.** These are expensive to remediate and may indicate ongoing structural issues.
- **Landlord unwilling to allow a pre-approval health inspection.** This is a reasonable request. Reluctance is a red flag.
- **Restricted hours.** Some commercial leases restrict operating hours. If you plan to open at 6am or operate late, confirm the lease allows it.
- **No clarity on who pays for HVAC maintenance or replacement.** HVAC systems in commercial kitchens work hard and fail. Make sure the lease is explicit.

## Lease Types Explained

Understanding your lease type determines your fixed cost model.

### Gross Lease
- You pay one flat monthly rent.
- The landlord pays property taxes, insurance, and maintenance.
- **Pros for tenant:** Predictable fixed cost. Good for early-stage businesses.
- **Cons:** Rent may be higher to compensate landlord for carrying those costs.
- **Common in:** Office buildings, some older retail strips.

### Triple Net (NNN) Lease
- You pay base rent PLUS your proportionate share of property taxes, building insurance, and common area maintenance (CAM charges).
- NNN charges can add 20–40% on top of the base rent and fluctuate year to year.
- **Pros for tenant:** Lower base rent headline number.
- **Cons:** True monthly cost is unpredictable. Carefully estimate NNN charges before comparing to gross leases.
- **Common in:** Most modern retail and strip mall spaces. Very common in food service.
- **What to ask:** Request 3 years of historical NNN charges from the landlord. Ask for a NNN cap.

### Modified Gross Lease
- A negotiated hybrid — tenant pays some operating expenses but not all.
- Common structure: tenant pays utilities; landlord pays taxes and insurance.
- **Pros:** More predictable than NNN; more affordable than full gross.
- **Common in:** Multi-tenant buildings, some mixed-use developments.

### What really matters for a food business
Food businesses have high fixed costs (rent, base labor, utilities) that don't flex with slow weeks. Your lease structure needs to:
1. Be predictable enough to model cash flow
2. Leave enough margin after rent to cover other fixed costs AND reach profitability
3. **Rule of thumb:** Rent should not exceed 8–12% of projected monthly revenue. Above 12% is risky for a food business.

## Key Lease Terms to Negotiate

Commercial leases are negotiable. Everything below is fair to ask for — landlords expect it.

### Tenant Improvement Allowance (TI)
The landlord contributes money toward your buildout costs in exchange for you signing a longer lease.

- **Typical range:** $20–$80/sq ft for retail food service. Higher in urban markets.
- **How it works:** Often structured as a credit against rent (first N months free) or a reimbursement after you've completed work.
- **Push for:** TI that covers at minimum your plumbing, electrical, and ventilation upgrades. These benefit the space long-term and a landlord who wants a strong tenant should contribute.

### Rent Abatement During Buildout
Free rent for the weeks/months between lease signing and opening.

- **Why it matters:** Your buildout will take 6–16 weeks minimum. You're paying rent on a space you can't use and revenue on yet. Abatement bridges this gap.
- **Typical ask:** 2–4 months of free rent, sometimes structured as 50% rent for a longer period.
- **Landlord perspective:** They'd rather give you free rent now than have you fail and vacate. A good landlord understands this.

### Personal Guarantee
Commercial leases almost always require a personal guarantee — you personally guarantee the lease payments even if the business fails.

- **This is a real risk.** If your LLC fails and you've personally guaranteed a 5-year lease, you owe the remaining rent.
- **Negotiate:**
  - **Cap the guarantee:** "Personal guarantee limited to 12 months of base rent" is a reasonable ask, especially for shorter leases.
  - **Sunset clause:** Guarantee expires after 2–3 years if you've paid on time.
  - **Burn-down:** Guarantee amount decreases as remaining lease term decreases.
- **If the landlord won't negotiate the guarantee:** Make sure your lease term isn't longer than you're comfortable personally guaranteeing.

### Exclusivity Clause
Prevents the landlord from renting to a direct competitor in the same property.

- **Ask for:** Exclusive right to operate a [bakery / café / food concept] in this property or shopping center.
- **Be specific:** Define what "competitor" means. "No other bakery" is better than "no other food business."
- **Landlords may resist for large multi-tenant properties.** A compromise: exclusivity within your building, or for a defined radius within the property.

### Assignment and Subletting Rights
Your right to transfer the lease to a buyer if you sell the business.

- **Why it matters:** If you build a successful business and want to sell it, the lease is a core asset. A buyer needs to be able to take over the lease.
- **Ask for:** Right to assign the lease without landlord approval (or with approval not to be unreasonably withheld) in connection with a sale of the business.
- **Landlords often want:** Approval rights over new tenant. This is reasonable; just ensure "approval not unreasonably withheld" is in the language.

### Renewal Options
Your right to renew the lease at a defined rate when your initial term ends.

- **Ask for:** One or two 5-year renewal options at a defined rent increase (e.g., CPI or 3% per year, capped at 10% per renewal period).
- **Why it matters:** You'll invest heavily in your space. You don't want to build a successful business and then have the landlord dramatically raise rent or refuse to renew.
- **Watch for:** Renewal options that "reset to market rate" — this gives you the right to stay but at whatever the landlord decides the market is.

### Use Clause
Defines what your space may be used for.

- **Make it broad:** "Retail food service, bakery, café, catering preparation, and any lawful related use" is better than "artisan bakery."
- **Why:** If your concept evolves (you add a catering arm, change your menu significantly, start selling packaged goods), a narrow use clause may require landlord approval or constitute a default.

## Red Flags in a Commercial Lease

Have an attorney review any lease before signing. These are specific things to watch for:

- **Unlimited personal guarantee with no cap or sunset** — significant personal financial exposure
- **No TI and no abatement** — you bear all buildout cost and all rent during buildout
- **"As-is" clause without disclosure** — you inherit any problems with the space; make sure you've done thorough due diligence
- **Landlord's right to terminate for redevelopment** — uncommon but devastating; avoid
- **Very broad "default" definitions** — some leases allow termination for minor non-monetary breaches; narrow the default triggers
- **No assignment rights** — your lease has no value if you want to sell
- **Prohibited use clauses that could block future expansion** — read carefully for anything that would prevent catering, alcohol, or outdoor seating if those are in your eventual shape

## Health Department Pre-Approval Site Visit

**Do this before signing any lease.**

Call your county health department and ask for a pre-approval or pre-application consultation for a food service establishment at the address you're considering. Most health departments offer this; some require it.

What they'll do:
- Walk the space with you (or review plans)
- Tell you what would need to change to meet code: plumbing additions, ventilation requirements, flooring replacement, equipment specifications
- Give you a rough sense of timeline and any red flags

What you'll learn:
- Whether the space is viable without a full gut renovation
- The cost range for required improvements (get contractor quotes before signing)
- How long the plan review and inspection process typically takes in your jurisdiction

This conversation is free, takes 30–60 minutes, and can prevent you from signing a lease on a space that would cost $50,000 to make code-compliant.

## Buildout Cost Ranges

Use these as rough planning figures. Get three contractor quotes before committing.

| Scenario | Cost per sq ft | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Light cosmetic (paint, fixtures, decor) | $20–$50 | Existing compliant kitchen infrastructure already in place |
| Moderate (flooring, counters, minor plumbing) | $50–$100 | Some existing food service infrastructure |
| Full food service buildout (shell space) | $100–$200 | Full kitchen plumbing, ventilation, electrical, flooring |
| Restaurant-grade full buildout (urban) | $200–$400+ | High-end markets; full custom kitchen; NYC/SF/LA etc. |

**What drives cost up:**
- Adding grease trap (line item: $5,000–$20,000)
- Adding hood ventilation system ($8,000–$25,000)
- Three-phase electrical upgrade ($5,000–$15,000)
- ADA restroom addition or modification ($8,000–$25,000)
- Any work requiring structural changes

**Lead times:** Commercial contractors are often booked 4–8 weeks out. Start soliciting bids as soon as you've identified your space — before you sign the lease if possible.

## How to Find Commercial Spaces

- **LoopNet.com** — largest commercial real estate listing database; free to search, listings are by commercial brokers
- **CoStar** — more complete database, typically requires a paid subscription; your commercial broker will have access
- **Local commercial real estate brokers** — they know what's available before it's listed, they negotiate on your behalf, and their fee is paid by the landlord (not you). Find one who specializes in retail food service.
- **Drive the neighborhood** — look for vacant spaces with "for lease" signs, especially those with existing food service infrastructure (hood exhausts, grease traps are visible indicators)
- **Ask local restaurant owners** — they often know which spaces are coming available in the area

## When to Hire a Commercial Real Estate Attorney

**Always, before signing.**

A commercial lease is a multi-year, six-figure commitment with personal guarantee exposure. A commercial real estate attorney reviewing your lease will:
- Identify problematic clauses
- Negotiate language changes
- Protect your assignment and renewal rights
- Review the personal guarantee terms

**Cost:** $500–$2,000 for a lease review and negotiation assistance. This is one of the highest-ROI professional fees you'll spend.

**Do not rely on the landlord's attorney.** They represent the landlord's interests. Do not rely on a general practice attorney for a commercial lease — find one who specializes in commercial real estate.
