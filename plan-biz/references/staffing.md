# Staffing Reference

Practical guidance for hiring, compensating, and managing staff at a small brick-and-mortar food business. Use this reference during the staffing plan session in `04-operations` and the hiring session in `05-setup`.

---

## When Do You Need Your First Hire?

Most bakery founders start as the sole operator. The trigger for your first hire is when one of these becomes true:
- You're regularly working more than 50 hours/week and can't keep up with demand
- You're turning away business because you can't produce enough
- You're spending time on tasks (counter service, packaging, cleaning) that prevent you from doing higher-value work (baking, business development)
- You're opening a storefront that requires front-of-house staff during all service hours

**Don't hire ahead of revenue.** Every hire is a fixed cost that you owe whether the store is busy or slow. Staff up incrementally — hire for the volume you have, not the volume you hope for.

## Roles for a Small Bakery

### Production Staff

**Head Baker / Pastry Chef**
- Responsible for all production: mixing, shaping, proofing, baking, finishing
- At a small operation, this is often the owner
- When to hire a head baker: when you want to step back from daily production OR when volume exceeds what one person can handle
- **Compensation:** $20–$28/hr (major metro markets run higher: $25–$35/hr)
- **What to look for:** Commercial kitchen experience, ability to work early hours (4–6am production starts are common), self-directed, quality-obsessed

**Prep Baker**
- Assists with production: measuring, mixing, portioning, cleanup
- Entry-level position; can be trained up from motivated generalists
- **Compensation:** $17–$22/hr
- **When to hire:** When your head baker is spending >30% of time on prep instead of skilled production

### Front-of-House Staff

**Counter Staff / Barista**
- Takes orders, manages display case, handles transactions, keeps the counter area clean
- If you serve coffee: add espresso training; this is a learnable skill but takes 2–4 weeks to develop proficiency
- **Compensation:** $15–$18/hr + tips (tips can add $3–$8/hr in a busy café environment)
- **When to hire:** Before opening day if your concept includes in-store service during your production hours

**Shift Lead / Keyholder**
- Experienced counter staff member trusted to open/close and handle issues without the owner present
- Usually promoted from within
- **Compensation:** $18–$22/hr; $1–$3/hr premium over base counter rate
- **When to hire:** Once you have 2+ counter staff and want to step away from opening/closing shifts

### Management

**Store Manager / General Manager**
- Owns day-to-day operations: scheduling, inventory, ordering, staff management, customer issues
- Typically hired once annual revenue exceeds $400–$600K or once you have 5+ employees
- **Compensation:** $45,000–$65,000/year salary; higher in major markets
- **What to look for:** Previous food service management experience, comfortable with numbers, strong with staff

### Production Support

**Dishwasher / Kitchen Assistant**
- Handles continuous dishwashing, floor sweeping, receiving deliveries, basic prep
- Often part-time; often the first hire at a growing operation
- **Compensation:** $15–$17/hr (minimum wage floor in many markets)

## Labor Cost Targets

**Target: 28–35% of revenue for total labor cost**

This includes:
- All hourly staff wages
- Your own compensation (if you're working in the store, your pay is a labor cost)
- Payroll taxes (employer pays ~7.65% of wages for Social Security + Medicare, plus state unemployment insurance — typically 1–5% depending on state)

**Rule of thumb:** When building your schedule, your total hourly labor budget is approximately (Monthly Revenue × 0.32) ÷ (average wage).

Example: $25,000/month revenue × 0.32 = $8,000 labor budget ÷ $18/hr average = 444 labor hours/month = ~15 labor hours/day.

If 15 hours/day isn't enough to run your operation, either your revenue needs to grow or your labor model needs to change.

## Compensation Benchmarks

| Role | Hourly Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Head Baker / Pastry Chef | $20–$28/hr | Higher in major metros |
| Prep Baker | $17–$22/hr | |
| Counter / Barista | $15–$18/hr + tips | Tips can add $3–$8/hr |
| Shift Lead | $18–$22/hr | |
| Dishwasher / Kitchen Asst | $15–$17/hr | Often minimum wage floor |
| Store Manager | $45K–$65K/year | Salary; larger markets run higher |

**Always verify your state and city minimum wage.** Federal minimum ($7.25) is a floor that most markets have long since exceeded. Many states and cities are at $15–$20+/hr.

**Tip wages:** Some states allow a "tip credit" — you can pay tipped employees below minimum wage as long as tips bring them to minimum. Many states (including California, Oregon, Washington, Minnesota) prohibit tip credits; all employees earn full minimum wage regardless of tips. Know your state's rule before setting wages.

## Hiring Timeline

Build this into your setup plan. Hiring takes longer than founders expect.

| Step | Timeline |
|---|---|
| Write job descriptions | 1–2 days |
| Post positions | Same day as ready |
| Receive applications | Ongoing; most come in first 1–2 weeks |
| Screen and schedule interviews | 1–2 weeks |
| Conduct interviews | 1–2 weeks |
| Background check (if required) | 3–7 business days |
| Offer and acceptance | 2–5 days |
| New hire paperwork (I-9, W-4, direct deposit) | Day 1 |
| ServSafe enrollment + training | 1–7 days |
| POS and SOP training | 3–7 days |
| Shadow shifts before solo | 3–10 shifts |
| **Total from posting to ready to work solo** | **4–6 weeks minimum** |

**Start hiring 6 weeks before your target opening date.** Most founders underestimate this timeline and end up opening understaffed or with undertrained staff.

## Required Training Before First Shift

**Every food handler must complete before handling food:**
- **Food handler card** (if required by your state): 2–4 hour online course; $7–$15 per person
- Your internal food safety orientation: proper handwashing, temperature danger zones (40°F–140°F), cross-contamination prevention, allergen awareness
- ServSafe certification for managers/shift leads (if applicable in your state): 1–2 day program + exam

**Every staff member must complete:**
- POS system training: how to ring up items, process returns, run end-of-day reports
- Menu knowledge: what everything is, ingredients, allergens, how it's made
- Opening and closing checklist walkthrough
- Cash handling procedures (if applicable)

**Role-specific training:**
- Counter / barista: espresso technique, drink menu, customer service standards
- Production: specific recipes, quality standards, food safety procedures, equipment operation
- Shift lead: opening/closing procedures, schedule management, handling customer complaints, cash deposits

## Scheduling Software

Manual scheduling (paper, text messages) breaks down quickly. Use software from day one.

| Tool | Best For | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| **7Shifts** | Food service focused; time clocking, tip tracking, labor cost reporting | $30–$100/mo depending on team size; free tier for very small teams |
| **When I Work** | Simple and affordable; good for small teams | $2.50–$6/person/month |
| **Deputy** | Strong compliance features; good for teams with varying availability | $3–$6/person/month |
| **Homebase** | Free for one location; good basic scheduling + time tracking | Free base plan |

**What to look for in scheduling software:**
- Mobile app for staff (shift notifications, shift swaps, availability updates)
- Time clock integration (clock in/out from POS or mobile)
- Labor cost visibility as you build the schedule
- Overtime alerts

## Onboarding Checklist

For every new hire, complete these items before or on their first day:

**Legal / Payroll (Day 1 — required)**
- [ ] I-9 (Employment Eligibility Verification) — must be completed within 3 business days of start; requires identity documents
- [ ] W-4 (Federal Income Tax Withholding)
- [ ] State withholding form
- [ ] Direct deposit authorization (or check preference)
- [ ] Add to payroll system

**Operational**
- [ ] Key / access code (if applicable)
- [ ] Uniform or dress code guidance
- [ ] Employee handbook receipt and signature
- [ ] ServSafe or food handler card enrollment
- [ ] Emergency contact form
- [ ] Scheduling system access

**Training**
- [ ] Food safety orientation (same day as first food contact)
- [ ] Menu walkthrough with tasting
- [ ] POS training
- [ ] Opening/closing checklist review
- [ ] Shadow shift with experienced staff member

## Employment Law Basics

**W-2 employees vs. 1099 contractors:** Counter staff, bakers, and kitchen workers are employees — not contractors. Misclassifying employees as contractors is a common and costly mistake. The IRS's test looks at behavioral control, financial control, and relationship type. If you set their hours, provide their equipment, and they work exclusively for you, they are employees.

**Overtime:** Non-exempt employees (all hourly food workers) earn 1.5× their regular rate for hours over 40/week. Some states (California, Colorado) also require daily overtime (over 8 hours/day). Never schedule around overtime by having staff clock out and keep working — this is wage theft and creates significant legal liability.

**Minor labor laws:** If you hire workers under 18, additional federal and state restrictions apply — hours they can work, equipment they can operate (e.g., commercial slicers), and roles they can hold. Check your state's minor labor laws before scheduling under-18 staff.

**Breaks and meals:** Many states require paid rest breaks (10 minutes per 4 hours) and unpaid meal breaks (30 minutes per 5–6 hours). Know your state's requirements.

**At-will employment:** Most states are at-will employment states — either party can end the employment relationship at any time for any lawful reason. Document performance issues anyway. A paper trail protects you if a termination is ever challenged.

## Handling Tips

**Cash tips:** Staff keep the cash they receive directly from customers (tip jar, handed tips). Document that tip jar proceeds are distributed to staff; do not keep tip jar proceeds as revenue.

**Credit card tips:** You collect them in your POS system and must distribute them to staff in a timely manner (most states: within the pay period they were earned). Your payroll system or accountant handles the tax reporting.

**Tip pools:** You can require staff to pool and share tips (e.g., splitting between counter and production). Management cannot participate in tip pools.

**Tip reporting:** Staff are required to report tip income to the IRS. As the employer, you may receive notices about tip reporting compliance. Use a payroll service to handle this correctly.

## Staff Retention: What Actually Works in Food Service

Food service has notoriously high turnover. Small businesses that retain staff usually do a few things:
- **Consistent schedules**: Staff with unpredictable schedules find other jobs. Give people the same shifts as much as possible.
- **Timely, accurate paychecks**: Payroll errors erode trust fast. Use a payroll service.
- **Clear advancement path**: Even in a small business, knowing there's a path to shift lead or manager matters.
- **Genuine appreciation**: Staff who feel seen and appreciated stay longer. Learn names, birthdays, what matters to them.
- **Reasonable hours**: Mandatory 5am–2pm five days a week burns people out. Build schedules that respect rest.
- **Training investment**: Staff who feel competent and proud of their work stay. Staff who feel set up to fail leave.
